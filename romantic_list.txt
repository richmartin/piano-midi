The 200 MIDI files in this group cover 70 works by Chopin, 47 works by Liszt and over 80 works by other composers
of the mid-1800s such as Mendelssohn, Saint-Saëns, Schubert and Schumann. Earliest recordings are from 1905.
Pianists include Wilhelm Backhaus, Harold Bauer, Ferruccio Busoni, Teresa Carreño, Alfred Cortot, Arthur Friedheim,
Ignaz Friedman, Leopold Godowsky, Josef Hofmann, Theodor Leschetizky, Mischa Levitzki, Josef Lhévinne, Benno
Moiseiwitsch, Vladimir de Pachmann, Ignace Paderewski, Sergei Rachmaninoff and Bernhard Stavenhagen.

A few highlights
• Barcarolle Op.39 No.1 in A-minor by Leschetizky, played by the composer
• Etude Heroique Op.48 No.3 by Leschetizky, played by Ervin Nyiregyházi
• Evening Harmonies (Harmonies du Soir) by Liszt, played by Arthur Friedheim
• Hungarian Rhapsody 10 by Liszt, played by Alfred Reisenauer*
• Hungarian Rhapsody 12 by Liszt, played by Bernhard Stavenhagen*
• Lilacs (Transcription) by Rachmaninoff, played by Sergei Rachmaninoff
• Nocturne Op.48 No.1, C minor by Chopin, played by Mischa Levitzki
• Scherzo Op.31 B flat minor by Chopin, played by Sergei Rachmaninoff
• Poeme Op.32 No.1 by Scriabin, played by the composer
• St Francis of Paula Walking on the Waves by Liszt, played by Bernhard Stavenhagen
• Benediction of God in Solitude by Liszt arr Siloti, played by Alexander Siloti.
* Rolls marked “as played by Liszt”

Background stories
1.

Arthur Friedheim (1859-1932) is mostly associated with Liszt, having been a pupil and also a famed interpreter of
Liszt’s works. The editor of Friedheim’s memoirs, Theodore Bullock says of Friedheim’s Duo-Art piano rolls that
these “do reasonable justice to his extraordinary endowments.”

2.

Alfred Reisenauer (1863-1907) was also a Liszt pupil, in which Liszt is reported to have said that “Reisenauer did
very closely approach his style, yet ‘without imitation’.” Pianist Clarence Adler (1886–1969) was a student of
Reisenauer’s and says he had an unparalleled tone production: “Such richness and quality! I used to think of gold.”

3.

Alfred Cortot (1877-1962) recorded an unusual performance of Liszt’s Hungarian Rhapsody No. 2, listed as
number 3099 in the British edition of the May 1927 Duo-Art Bulletin. It is likely this roll recording was not widely
circulated and is therefore a rare recording.

4.

Josef Lhévinne (1874–1944) recorded 21 works for Ampico and 27 works on Welte-Mignon and Licensee roll. He
made just over an hour of sound recordings between the years 1920–37. Schonberg explains that Lhévinne
“belonged in the company of giants of any age and this despite the fact that he was not one of the international
headliners.” He appears in this library in nearly all categories, playing 11 works in this category.

5.

Mieczyslaw Münz (1900-1976) entered the Krakow Conservatory at age nine, later moving to Berlin, becoming
part of the ‘Busoni circle’ where at age 20 he played three concertos in one evening with the Berlin Symphony
Orchestra. His career was curtailed in the early 1940s by a medical problem in his right hand. His pupils include
Emanuel Ax, Alan Feinberg, Walter Hautzig, Ann Schein and Ilana Vered.

6.

Carl Reinecke (1824-1910) is the earliest-born musician of any genre to make any form of recording. He recorded
only on piano roll in 1905-07. He was a dominant musical figure in Germany over his life.
